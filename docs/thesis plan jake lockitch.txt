Thesis title: (Working title of the thesis)
Improving Robustness And Practical Viability Of Ai-Based Cctv Violence/Incident Detection Under Township-Like
Conditions In South Africa
Description of the assignment: (A single page, describing the assignment and indications of the techniques that will
be explored)
This thesis evaluates whether AI-based CCTV violence, crime and aggression detection can be technically and
operationally feasible in South African townships. In practice CCTV footage in townships involves low
resolution, low light, blocked crowd and objects and has to deal with frequent power outages (loadshedding). These factors directly cause a "transferability" issue: a model may perform very well with
controlled training, but results can degrade hugely under real-world conditions.
Therefore, we will go further than just applying pre-trained models in a township. The goal is to create
technical novelty by coming up with and evaluating an adapted architecture and training approach to
improve robustness and accuracy in township contexts. The work will be conducted using public CCTV-style
datasets tagged with violence vs non-violence / incident vs non-incident. This enables reproducible
evaluation without requiring sensitive local CCTV data.
This thesis will consist of four components:
Mapping existing approaches and evidence quality: Conduct a structured review of AI-enabled CCTV
violence/incident detection research and reported deployments. Extract model families, data assumptions
and metrics used. Distinguish benchmark-only evidence from field evaluations.
Baseline implementation and evaluation on public annotated datasets: Reproduce at least one
representative baseline model for video violence/incident detection. Establish a transparent evaluation
pipeline and report baseline results.
Township robustness benchmark via controlled degradations: Create a township-like stress test benchmark
by systematically degrading the videos (down sampling, compression, low-light, blur etc). Quantify the
decrease in performance and analyse failure models focusing on false positives. This benchmark will be
referred to as the Township Corruption Benchmark and is a core contribution of this thesis, inspired by video
corruption benchmarks. Chang et al. (2020) study realistic video file corruptions and report large accuracy
drops in action recognition, motivating corruption-oriented evaluation beyond clean benchmarks.
Technically novel robustness improvement and viability assessment: Propose and evaluate an adapted
architecture and training method aimed at improving robustness under degradations. Compare results
against baselines and report improvements. Robustness training will be included by training with both clean
and degraded data. Similar to corruption-aware augmentation strategies like PRIME (Modas et al., 2022).
Datasets (public, annotated and CCTV-style):
Bus Violence (Ciampi et al., 2022): 1400 videos (700 violence/700 non-violence) recorded from three
cameras in a bus. Variation of lighting and backgrounds.
Real-Life Violence Situations (RLVS): 2000 videos collected from real world scenes.
Surveillance Camera Fight dataset: 300 videos (150 fight / 150 non-fight). Surveillance footage.
Baseline models/architecture: I3D (inflated 3D ConvNet), SlowFast Networks. 3D CNNs are strong baselines
on violence datasets and have been used in Bus Violence experiments (Ciampi et al., 2022). Along with a 3D
CNN baseline, a modern transformer baseline will be included to compare robustness behaviour under
corruptions. For the baseline comparison, I will pick only one model for each family: SlowFast Networks as
the 3D CNN baseline (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) and Video Swin Transformer as the transformer baseline
(Liu et al., 2022).

Standard evaluation: training/validate/test on each dataset using official splits when available or use
(70/15/15) with a random seed then report baseline performance.
Township-like robustness benchmark: edit the video quality down sample / compress / reduce brightness /
motion blur / occlusions / reduce FPS, on 3 levels: mild, medium and severe. Township corruption taxonomy
examples: spatial quality: resolution down sampling (e.g. 224→112→64). JPEG/MPEG compression (different
bitrates) / motion blur / low-light / reduced FPS (simulate cheap cameras and dropped frames) and scenelevel: synthetic occlusion (random rectangles, blocking view), mild camera shake.
Technical novelty: propose and evaluate an adapted system and training approach to improve robustness
under township like scenarios, for example adding a quality-aware component to deal with low video
quality. Compare the adapted system against the baseline models on both clean and degraded videos and
check if robustness improved without an unacceptable increase in false positives.
Evaluation: report accuracy, precision, recall and F1 on each dataset. Also ROC/AUC and confusion matrices
looking specifically at false positives. Evaluate clean vs degraded videos (mild, medium and severe) for each
corruption type, and summarise results in a table showing performance of each model under each condition
so an answer can be given about the extent to which this problem can be solved under various conditions.

Problem statement: (Single sentence describing the overall challenge faced by the student)
AI-based CCTV violence/incident detection often shows high benchmark performance, but its reliability and
performance decreases under low-quality, crowded, and resource-constrained conditions, making it unclear whether
it can be viable in township-like environments without targeted robustness improvements.
Research questions: (Provide two to five research questions of a single sentence that by answering them, jointly
provide a path for addressing the problem statement)
1. How do representative baseline CCTV violence/incident detection models perform on public annotated
CCTV-style datasets under standard evaluation, and what patterns of false positives and false negatives
emerge?
2. To what extent does performance degrade when the same models are evaluated under township-like video
conditions (e.g., low resolution, low-light, compression, blur, occlusion, reduced FPS), and which failure
modes are most common at different severity levels?
3. Can an adapted architecture or robustness-oriented training strategy improve accuracy and stability under
degraded conditions compared to baselines, without causing unacceptable increases in false alarms,
computational cost, or inference latency?
4. Which specific degradation types contribute most to real-world transferability failures, and which mitigation
strategies provide the best trade-off between performance on clean footage and robustness against
degraded township-like conditions (especially in terms of false positives vs. missed incidents)?
